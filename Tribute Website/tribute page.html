A. P. J. Abdul Kalam

(15 October 1931 – 27 July 2015)

Missile Man: Dr. A.P.J. Abdul Kalam

Dr. A.P.J. Abdul Kalam, widely known as the "Missile Man of India," was a renowned aerospace scientist and the 11th President of India. Born on October 15, 1931, in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu, he rose from humble beginnings to become one of the most respected leaders and scientists in the country. His work in the Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO) and the Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) significantly advanced India's missile and space programs. Known for his simplicity, vision, and dedication to national development, Dr. Kalam inspired millions, particularly the youth, through his speeches, writings, and personal example.

Achievements of Dr. A.P.J. Abdul Kalam

Dr. Kalam played a pivotal role in India’s missile development programs, including the successful launch of Agni and Prithvi missiles, earning him the title “Missile Man of India.” He was also a key figure in India’s Pokhran-II nuclear tests in 1998. As the chief architect of the Integrated Guided Missile Development Programme (IGMDP), he helped India achieve self-reliance in defense technologies. In addition to his scientific accomplishments, he served as the President of India from 2002 to 2007, becoming a beloved “People’s President.” His numerous awards include the Bharat Ratna, Padma Bhushan, and Padma Vibhushan. Beyond science and politics, Dr. Kalam was a prolific writer and a passionate advocate for education and youth empowerment.

About Abdul Kalam

Born and raised in a Muslim family in Rameswaram, Tamil Nadu, Kalam studied physics and aerospace engineering. He spent the next four decades as a scientist and science administrator, mainly at the Defence Research and Development Organisation (DRDO) and Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO) and was intimately involved in India's civilian space programme and military missile development efforts. He was known as the "Missile Man of India" for his work on the development of ballistic missile and launch vehicle technology. He also played a pivotal organisational, technical, and political role in Pokhran-II nuclear tests in 1998, India's second such test after the first test in 1974. Kalam was elected as the president of India in 2002 with the support of both the ruling Bharatiya Janata Party and the then-opposition Indian National Congress. He was widely referred to as the "People's President". He engaged in teaching, writing and public service after his presidency. He was a recipient of several awards, including the Bharat Ratna, India's highest civilian honour. While delivering a lecture at IIM Shillong, Kalam collapsed and died from an apparent cardiac arrest on 27 July 2015, aged 83. Thousands attended the funeral ceremony held in his hometown of Rameswaram, where he was buried with full state honours. A memorial was inaugurated near his home town in 2017.
